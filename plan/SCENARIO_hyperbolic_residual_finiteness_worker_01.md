# Scenario: Hyperbolic Residual Finiteness Counterexample Worker 01

This note is a counterexample-search plan for the open problem:

```math
\text{Is every word-hyperbolic group residually finite?}
```

It is deliberately conservative. No item below is claimed to solve the problem. Each route lists:

1. the exact theorem-shaped input that would produce a counterexample;
2. the known machinery that gets close;
3. the obstruction that prevents the route from already being a counterexample.

## Baseline facts to keep fixed

Let

```math
R_f(G)=\bigcap_{[G:N]<\infty,\ N\triangleleft G}N
```

be the finite residual. A negative example is a finitely generated word-hyperbolic group
`G` with `R_f(G) != 1`.

Known reductions and warning signs:

- Kapovich--Wise: residual finiteness for all word-hyperbolic groups is equivalent to the
  assertion that every nontrivial word-hyperbolic group has a nontrivial finite quotient.
- Agol--Groves--Manning: existence of a non-residually finite hyperbolic group is equivalent
  to existence of a nonseparable quasiconvex subgroup of some hyperbolic group.
- Haglund--Wise / Agol--Wise technology proves residual finiteness and quasiconvex subgroup
  separability for broad virtually special classes. Therefore any proposed counterexample must
  be checked very early against virtual specialness/cubulation results.
- Finite-kernel extensions preserve hyperbolicity up to quasi-isometry, but do not preserve
  residual finiteness. This is the opening exploited by Deligne-type arithmetic constructions.

## Route A: Rips / small-cancellation / Dehn-filling

### The tempting input

Rips-type constructions produce, for a finitely presented group `Q`, a short exact sequence

```math
1 \to N \to G \to Q \to 1
```

where `G` is small-cancellation and word-hyperbolic, and `N` is finitely generated.

Variations can force additional features: residually finite Rips groups in Wise's version,
property-(T) kernels in Ollivier--Wise, cubical versions, and relative versions.

### Why this does not already give a counterexample

Choosing `Q` non-residually finite does not imply `G` is non-residually finite: residual
finiteness does not pass to quotients. Wise's residually finite Rips construction is the
explicit warning example: even bad `Q` can be the quotient of residually finite `G`.

Choosing `Q` with no nontrivial finite quotients also does not force an element of `G` into
`R_f(G)`. In a finite quotient of `G`, the image of `Q` may be trivial while the finite quotient
is generated by the image of `N`. To prove non-residual finiteness one needs a specific
nontrivial element `g in G` killed by every finite quotient; Rips alone gives no such element.

Dehn filling gives hyperbolic quotients after sufficiently long fillings, but the usual
separating arguments require deep finite-index subgroups in peripheral or quasiconvex subgroups.
That is exactly where residual finiteness/separability enters, so a naive filling proof becomes
circular.

### Concrete subtask

Work in an explicit graphical `C'(1/6)` or cubical Rips presentation

```math
1 \to N \to G \to Q \to 1
```

with `Q` finitely presented and with no nontrivial finite quotients. Compute the induced
constraints on finite quotients of `G`. The target is not merely "all maps factor through `N`",
but:

```math
\exists 1 \ne g \in N \quad \forall \phi:G\to F \text{ finite},\quad \phi(g)=1.
```

Current gap: no known mechanism forces such a uniform element while preserving hyperbolicity.

## Route B: arithmetic finite-kernel extensions and congruence kernels

### The tempting input

Deligne constructed non-residually finite central extensions of higher-rank arithmetic groups,
using the congruence subgroup property. Later formulations give: for suitable simple,
algebraically simply connected `Q`-groups with positive real rank and finite congruence kernel,
arithmetic subgroups admit finite abelian extensions

```math
1 \to A \to \widetilde{\Gamma} \to \Gamma \to 1
```

with `\widetilde{\Gamma}` not residually finite.

If `\Gamma` is a uniform lattice in a rank-one negatively curved Lie group, then `\Gamma` is
word-hyperbolic; since the kernel `A` is finite, `\widetilde{\Gamma}` is also word-hyperbolic.
Thus:

```math
\text{uniform rank-one arithmetic lattice}
+ \text{finite congruence kernel}
+ \text{Deligne-type non-RF extension}
\Longrightarrow
\text{hyperbolic non-RF group}.
```

### Why this does not already give a counterexample

The missing input is precisely the dangerous one: finite congruence kernel in rank one. Serre's
rank-one congruence-kernel expectation is the opposite direction: the congruence kernel should be
infinite. Published results also show infinite congruence kernels in important rank-one
nonarchimedean cases.

A second trap is nonuniform lattices. Finite-volume noncompact real-hyperbolic manifolds in
dimension at least `3` contain higher-rank cusp subgroups such as `Z^2`; their groups are
relatively hyperbolic but not word-hyperbolic. For the present problem, use uniform lattices or
low-dimensional exceptions only.

### Concrete subtask

Pick a specific compact arithmetic lattice `Gamma < Spin(n,1)` or `SU(n,1)` arising from a
simply connected `Q`-group. For a chosen finite central cover, compute the restriction class

```math
\alpha \in H^2(\Gamma;A)
```

and test whether every finite-index subgroup keeps a nontrivial restriction of `alpha`. If yes,
the preimage extension is a candidate non-RF hyperbolic group. Then check whether the required
non-virtual-splitting is already ruled out by known rank-one congruence-kernel infinitude or by
known residual-finiteness/linearity results for that lattice class.

Current gap: no known compact rank-one arithmetic lattice is known to satisfy the finite
congruence-kernel input needed for Deligne's obstruction.

## Route C: common quotients, property (T), and finite-quotient scarcity

### The tempting input

Olshanskii quotient machinery and common-quotient theorems show that non-elementary hyperbolic
groups have extremely flexible quotients. Random and graphical small-cancellation methods also
produce many hyperbolic groups with property (T).

Property (T) is attractive because it blocks many cubulation strategies and imposes rigidity on
linear representations. If one could prove that a nontrivial property-(T) hyperbolic group has no
nontrivial finite quotient, then Kapovich--Wise would immediately give a negative answer.

### Why this does not already give a counterexample

Property (T) does not obstruct finite quotients; finite groups themselves have property (T), and
many known property-(T) groups have many finite quotients. Random groups at density `<1/6` are
known residually finite via cubulation and virtual specialness. At densities where property (T)
is typical, residual finiteness/non-residual finiteness is not known by current general methods.

Olshanskii-style infinite quotients with few or no finite-index subgroups are often obtained as
limits or as finitely presented quotients not known to remain word-hyperbolic. Infinite Burnside
quotients are not word-hyperbolic counterexamples: an infinite finitely generated
word-hyperbolic torsion group cannot arise in the naive way, because hyperbolic groups have
uniformly bounded finite subgroups and strong torsion constraints.

### Concrete subtask

Use a short-presentation census of hyperbolic property-(T) groups. For each presentation:

1. certify hyperbolicity or a small-cancellation condition;
2. enumerate homomorphisms to finite simple groups up to a growing size/rank cutoff;
3. separately test whether cubulation/virtual-special arguments apply;
4. look for a structural proof that a named generator lies in every finite quotient.

Current gap: finite-quotient scarcity is experimentally approachable but no proof presently
turns it into `R_f(G) != 1`.

## Route D: nonseparable quasiconvex subgroup equivalence

### Exact counterexample target

By Agol--Groves--Manning, the following are equivalent:

```math
\exists\ \text{non-RF word-hyperbolic group}
```

and

```math
\exists\ (G,H,g)
\quad
G\ \text{word-hyperbolic},\quad
H<G\ \text{quasiconvex},\quad
g\notin H,
\quad
\forall \phi:G\to F\ \text{finite},\ \phi(g)\in \phi(H).
```

This is arguably the cleanest route because it replaces an invisible element of the finite
residual by a visible nonseparability witness.

### Why this does not already give a counterexample

Every known candidate must survive the virtual-special filter. If `G` is hyperbolic virtually
special, all quasiconvex subgroups are separable. This eliminates many cubulated small
cancellation groups, Coxeter groups, many arithmetic hyperbolic manifolds of simplest type, and
3-manifold groups covered by Agol--Wise machinery.

Rips subgroups are often non-quasiconvex. They can be finitely generated and pathological, but
nonseparability of a non-quasiconvex subgroup is irrelevant to the Agol--Groves--Manning
equivalence.

### Concrete subtask

Search outside currently certified virtually special classes for an explicit pair `(G,H)` where:

- `G` has a small finite presentation with a certified hyperbolicity proof;
- `H` is generated by a short list and has an independent quasiconvexity certificate;
- a chosen `g notin H` survives all low-index subgroup tests as lying in the profinite closure
  of `H`;
- no known cubulation/virtual-special theorem applies.

Current gap: no known explicit hyperbolic `G` with quasiconvex nonseparable `H`.

## Ranked action list

1. **Arithmetic finite-kernel extension audit.** This is the sharpest theorem-to-counterexample
   route: one finite congruence-kernel rank-one input would produce a hyperbolic non-RF finite
   extension. The task is to audit compact rank-one arithmetic lattices and determine exactly
   where Serre's conjectural infinitude blocks Deligne's argument.
2. **Quasiconvex nonseparability witness search.** This is equivalent to the main problem and
   gives a concrete certificate `(G,H,g)`. Prioritize property-(T) or otherwise non-cubulated
   hyperbolic presentations.
3. **Finite quotient census for property-(T) hyperbolic groups.** Use computation only as a
   guide. A proof must identify a nontrivial element in every finite-quotient kernel, not merely
   fail to find quotients below a bound.
4. **Rips/Dehn-filling obstruction engineering.** Useful as a source of controlled pathology,
   but currently the least direct: the finite quotients of the Rips kernel can absorb all finite
   images, and Dehn-filling separability arguments tend to use the residual finiteness one is
   trying to refute.
